The Fast Lane

When I started high school, I was immediately shocked by the fast pace, lack of intimacy, and workload. It wasn’t unmanageable in freshman year, but afterwards I always felt like I was drowning in responsibilities that no one warned me about. I went to one of those suburban schools where “excellence is expected” and anything less is seen as lazy and unmotivated.No one talked about mental health and it’s hard to start a conversation when everyone just seemed fine. I think sophomore year is when I started to get depressed. I missed meals, slacked on work, didn’t go out with friends. It felt like the world was moving so fast but I was stuck in slow-motion.

Looking back on it now, I realize that I probably wasn’t the only one who felt like this, but that’s not what I thought at the time. Since no one told me that high school wasn’t as easy as middle school (in fact it was 10x harder) and no one validated my emotions or even let me express them, I felt like I was alone in this. Junior year came and with it, the stress of college, SAT’s, and being a failure. When I didn’t score as high as my parent’s liked me too, and I got my first ever D, I became volatile. I began to think, ‘what if this is it? What if this is what the rest of my life is like?’ And for a moment, I considered how good it would feel to just escape all of it and never have to deal with life’s tribulations again. That was probably my lowest point.

I am better now, and I have seeked help in the form of therapy, but not everyone is as fortunate. Schools are a haven for learning and growing, but where there is fresh water there is always bacteria, algae, and all sorts of nasty stuff. Bullying, emotional abuse, and overload symptoms thrive in a high school environment. And without proper education and mediation when it comes to mental health, they will continue to thrive in an environment full of malleable, naive teenagers. Not only should we be discussing these issues, but we should be taking preventative steps, ensuring that no one will have to endure what I had to go through, or worse. High school is a time for identity, growth, and learning. But how can that occur when obstacles impede teenagers every day? We must learn to fix this problem as a society, or else high school will just be that place that causes 3% of teens to kill themselves.
